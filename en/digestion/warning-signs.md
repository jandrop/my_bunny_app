# Warning Signs

This guide helps you quickly identify when your rabbit needs urgent veterinary attention.

---

## URGENT - Immediate Vet Visit

Go to the vet **immediately** if you observe:

| Sign | Why It's Serious |
|------|------------------|
| Not eating for more than **8-12 hours** | Could be GI stasis |
| No droppings or very small ones | Digestive system stopped |
| Difficulty breathing | Severe respiratory problem |
| Head tilt | Possible ear infection or E. cuniculi |
| Extreme lethargy / not moving | Intense pain or serious illness |
| Excessive drooling | Severe dental problems |
| Hard abdominal swelling | Intestinal blockage |
| Blood in urine or feces | Infection or internal injury |
| Seizures | Neurological emergency |
| Temperature > 40°C (104°F) or < 37°C (98.6°F) | Fever or hypothermia |

---

## Watch Closely

These signs require close monitoring and possible vet visit:

- Partial loss of appetite.
- Droppings smaller than normal.
- Less activity than usual.
- Matted or unkempt fur.
- Frequent sneezing.
- Watery eyes or discharge.
- Loud teeth grinding (pain).
- Limping or difficulty moving.

---

## Dangerous Toys and Materials

Watch that your rabbit **DOES NOT ingest**:

### Remove Immediately If You See Them Chewing:
- Laminated cardboard or with toxic ink
- Synthetic fabrics (polyester, nylon)
- Rubber bands
- Plastics
- Foams
- Synthetic ropes

### Safe to Chew:
- Brown cardboard without ink
- Newspaper (small amounts)
- Untreated wood (apple, willow)
- Natural wicker
- Pressed hay

---

## Daily Health Checklist

Check these points every day:

### Eating
- [ ] Is eating hay normally?
- [ ] Drinking water as usual?
- [ ] Accepts vegetables eagerly?

### Droppings
- [ ] Droppings of normal size?
- [ ] Usual amount of droppings?
- [ ] No diarrhea or mucus?

### Behavior
- [ ] Active and curious?
- [ ] Moving without difficulty?
- [ ] Responds when you talk to them?

### Physical Appearance
- [ ] Eyes clean and bright?
- [ ] Nose dry and clean?
- [ ] Coat clean and well-groomed?

---

## When to Call the Vet

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Emergency (red list above) | Go **immediately** |
| Warning signs | Call for consultation |
| Doubt about behavior | Observe 24h, then call |
| Routine checkup | Annual minimum |

---

## Important Tips

- **Know the schedule** of your exotic animal vet.
- Have a **24-hour emergency vet** located.
- **Don't wait** if you have doubts → better safe than sorry.
- Rabbits **hide pain** → when they show it, it's serious.
- Save your vet's number in your phone.
